Lead Story · The Portfolio, Properly Filed

Grade 12, eighty-one repositories, two reported bugs, and a decade of not waiting for permission.

Zenith Kandel is seventeen, a senior at Kathmandu Model Secondary School in Bagbazar, and has been filing commits — and the occasional vulnerability disclosure — from a small apartment above the capital since a pandemic handed him a laptop and too much free time.

Filed by the subject, with reluctance • Photographs: engravings on the web • Bagbazar, this morning

Every portfolio on the internet, this one included, begins with an attempt to compress a life into a few neat paragraphs. Let us try anyway. In the autumn of 2019, a ten-year-old in Bagbazar opened a YouTube tab, copied his first <h1> from a tutorial, and discovered the slightly absurd truth that with a text file and a browser you can build a small piece of the world.

Seven years on, the text file has become a habit. The repository count on GitHub is now north of eighty. The project list includes a crowd-priced index for Nepali bazaars, a long-range radio for when the cell towers blink off, a Kathmandu bus router, a soil sensor that argues with farmers, and an RFID attendance system for schools that still take roll call by shouting names into a courtyard. Two of those projects have won hackathons. Two of them, more quietly, won the attention of a security team at a national television channel and at one of the most-used typing tools on the internet.

He is, by his own description, still learning. He is also, by the evidence, hard to keep up with — currently moving from PHP and MySQL into the MERN stack, and reading bug reports in the same week he submits his homework. Theodicy is not on the syllabus. The work, however, is.

“It started as lockdown boredom. It became a way of paying attention.” — attributed, very loosely, to the editor

Section II · Works & Expeditions

Five Expeditions from the Hackathon Trenches

The following are projects submitted, demoed, and — in two cases — judged worthy of a small trophy and a slightly larger certificate. Filed chronologically, with the usual disclaimers.

N° 01

Crowd-Sourced Price Index · Web App

Sastomahango

n. the small, civic-minded art of not getting overcharged for rice.

Sastomahango is a crowd-sourced price index for daily goods — built for a country where haggling is the default and receipts are not. Users submit what they actually paid, others check before they buy, and the wisdom of the bazaar accumulates in a small, queryable ledger. A modest, useful, mildly subversive civic tool.

  • Filed under hackathon · civic tech
  • Stack HTML · CSS · JS · PHP · MySQL
  • Verdict award · applause, also
N° 02

Off-Grid RF · Hardware & Firmware

Lifeline

n. a small box of electronics, a large moral argument.

Lifeline is a low-power, off-grid radio-frequency emergency signal system, designed for the parts of Nepal where the mobile network has no opinion — the landslide, the flood, the hill, the night. It is the kind of project that looks, in photographs, like a handful of components and reads, in practice, like a polite request that nobody be left out of the conversation.

  • Filed under hackathon · emergency tech
  • Stack RF modules · embedded C · antennas, mostly improvised
  • Verdict built under pressure, still works
N° 03

Public Transit Router · Web App

Sawari

n. a love letter to a chaotic system, written by a frequent rider.

Sawari takes a starting point and a destination inside Kathmandu and returns, in plain language, the entire choreography of getting from one to the other on a public bus — fare, ETA, road conditions, the microbend where you change from the 12 to the 21. It is, in essence, Google Maps for a city that runs on conductors shouting at helpers.

  • Filed under hackathon · urban transit
  • Stack HTML · CSS · JS · PHP · MySQL
  • Verdict awarded, then used by the author daily
N° 04

Soil Monitor · IoT & Recommendation

AgroPan

n. soil that talks back, in plain language.

AgroPan combines in-ground sensors with a small web application to monitor soil moisture, nutrients, and pH, and to recommend — in language a farmer can act on — what to do next. It is one of the rare projects that crosses the line from app into thing you can put in a field, and it carries the marks of that journey: cable ties, data sheets, and real dirt.

  • Filed under exhibition · agri-tech
  • Stack sensors · microcontrollers · JS dashboard · MySQL
  • Verdict exhibited, deployed, still being calibrated
N° 05

RFID Attendance · Hardware & Web

Edu Track Pro

n. replacing the morning roll call with a small piece of plastic.

Edu Track Pro is an RFID-based attendance system built for Nepali schools, where the alternative to automation is shouting names into a courtyard. The hardware is simple, the data is useful, and the result is that a teacher can take attendance in roughly the time it used to take to sharpen a pencil.

  • Filed under school · ed-tech
  • Stack RFID · microcontrollers · PHP · MySQL
  • Verdict shipped to a real classroom, briefly, in pride
Section III · Disclosed Vulnerabilities

A Record of Uninvited Guests

The author, in his spare time, looks at things. Sometimes, gently, the things look back. The following entries are public, responsible, and, as far as can be confirmed, both fixed and acknowledged.

  1. 01

    Web Application · National Broadcaster

    Kantipur Television

    A security issue identified on the website of one of Nepal’s largest news channels. Reported through the appropriate channel, acknowledged, and quietly patched. No bounty was requested. None was offered. The work, however, was done.

    • Type Web application flaw
    • Status Fixed · acknowledged
    • Disposition responsible disclosure, full stop
  2. 02

    Open Source Tool · Used by Millions

    Monkeytype

    A flaw found in one of the most popular typing tools on the internet, used by everyone from competitive typists to people who simply like a clean keyboard sound. The author found something he shouldn’t have been able to. He reported it. They patched it. He did not, for the record, ask for money.

    • Type Web application flaw
    • Status Fixed · acknowledged
    • Disposition responsible disclosure, no bounty, no hard feelings
